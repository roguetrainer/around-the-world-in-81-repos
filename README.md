### The New Core Competencies

Look through these repositories and you'll find plenty of cruftâ€”inconsistent naming, redundant functions, documentation drift, architectural decisions that made sense at 2am but not at 2pm. Refactoring and tidying this will take hours of patient human-AI collaboration. 

**And that's the skill we all need to master**: 

- **Context window management**: What information does the AI need right now? What can be summarized? What must be preserved verbatim?
- **Project archaeology**: Reconstructing the reasoning behind past decisions when you're the one who made them three weeks ago
- **Conversational choreography**: Knowing when to be precise, when to be vague, when to ask for alternatives, when to just say "make it work"
- **Institutional memory**: Keeping records of who asked what, when decisions were made, why approaches were abandoned
- **Quality control**: Recognizing when AI output is brilliant, when it's plausible-but-wrong, and when it's hallucinated nonsense
- **Strategic direction**: The AI can generate implementations all day, but *you* decide what problems are worth solving

### The Humbling Truth

These 81 repositories don't showcase my coding prowessâ€”they showcase (hopefully some!) **effective collaboration with AI tools**. That's not a lesser skill; it's the *essential* skill for modern technical work. 

The bottleneck isn't typing speed or memorizing APIs anymore. It's:
- Asking the right questions
- Providing the right context
- Recognizing good output from bad
- Maintaining coherence across multiple sessions
- Knowing which problems to tackle and in what order

This is the new literacy: **working effectively with AI collaborators while keeping your human judgment engaged**.

The code in these repos? Any competent LLM could write it. The *collection* of these 81 repos, covering this particular breadth, exploring these specific trade-offs, making these connections? That required human intention, curiosity, and direction.

That's the skill worth developing.

## Sample Projects from the Journey

Here are examples of repositories created during this exploration:

### Quantum Computing
- **Quantum Error Correction Frameworks**: Implementation of surface codes, stabilizer formalism, and syndrome decoding (Qiskit, Q#)
- **Quantum Dominoes**: Educational game exploring quantum measurement and superposition (PennyLane)
- **Quantum Finance Models**: David Orrell's quantum approaches to option pricing and market dynamics (Python)

### Economics & Finance
- **Stock-Flow Consistent (SFC) Models**: Canadian economic modeling with sectoral balance sheets (Python, R)
- **Godley-Lavoie SFC Implementations**: Core macroeconomic models from monetary economics (Python)
- **Quantum Money Models**: Information-theoretic approaches to monetary systems (Q#, Qiskit)
- **Factor Models**: BARRA-style fundamental factor risk models (F#, Python)

### Statistical & Mathematical Physics
- **Path Integral Monte Carlo**: Quantum statistical mechanics simulations (Python)
- **Renormalization Group Methods**: Scale-dependent phenomena in physics and economics (Python, Julia)
- **Stochastic Differential Equations**: Numerical methods for SDEs in finance and physics (Python, R)

### AI & Machine Learning
- **AI Literacy Framework**: Educational resources for understanding modern AI systems (Python, JavaScript)
- **Graph Neural Networks**: Implementations for molecular and financial networks (PyTorch)
- **Variational Quantum Eigensolvers**: Hybrid quantum-classical optimization (Qiskit, PennyLane)
- **Bayesian Neural Networks**: Probabilistic deep learning for uncertainty quantification (PyMC, TensorFlow)

### Computational Methods
- **Finite Element Analysis**: PDE solvers for physical systems (Python)
- **Monte Carlo Methods**: From basic sampling to advanced MCMC techniques (Python, R, SQL)
- **Numerical Linear Algebra**: Matrix decompositions and iterative solvers (Python, F#)

### Data & Visualization
- **Interactive Economic Dashboards**: Web-based SFC model explorers (JavaScript, D3.js)
- **Financial Time Series Analysis**: GARCH, volatility modeling, regime switching (R, Python)
- **Network Analysis**: Financial contagion and systemic risk (NetworkX, SQL)

## The New Mythical Man-Month

Oh my! - a half century since [*The Mythical Man-Month: Essays on Software Engineering*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month) was published in 1975!

Fred Brooks famously argued that adding people to a late software project makes it later, because communication overhead scales quadratically. But LLM collaborators change this calculus:

- **Zero onboarding time**: Context provided through prompts
- **Instant context switching**: Move between quantum physics and finance seamlessly
- **Parallel exploration**: Multiple implementations can be developed simultaneously
- **Polyglot fluency**: Write Python, then R, then F# without cognitive overhead
- **No ego or politics**: Just focus on the problem

This doesn't make human expertise less valuableâ€”it makes it *more* valuable by freeing us to focus on what we do best: asking good questions, providing domain knowledge, making creative connections, and choosing the right tools for the job.
